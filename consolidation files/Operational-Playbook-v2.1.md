## PHASE 0 — WEEKS 1 THROUGH 4
### Legal Foundation
*Complete before anything else — no flexibility in sequencing*

**Build the container before you fill it**

Everything else in this playbook depends on Phase 0 being completed correctly. You cannot apply for government grants as an individual. You cannot sign a licensing agreement that holds up in court as an individual. You cannot transfer the patent to a company that does not yet exist. Phase 0 is the one phase with no flexibility in sequencing — it must be done first, and done properly.

### 0.1 Incorporate Bridgepoint Intelligence Inc.

Engage a startup-focused technology lawyer in Vancouver or Toronto to incorporate a federal corporation under the Canada Business Corporations Act. Do not use a bare-minimum online incorporation service — the share structure matters and getting it wrong now means an expensive restructuring later. The lawyer will set up two classes of shares: common shares for you as the founder, and a blank-cheque preferred share class that can be configured when investors arrive. This flexibility costs nothing to set up now and saves significant legal fees if you ever raise external capital.

> **⚖️ Specific instruction for the lawyer:**
>
> Tell them explicitly: (1) federal incorporation under CBCA, (2) dual share class structure with common and blank-cheque preferred, (3) you as sole director and officer initially, (4) registered office in British Columbia, (5) articles that permit the company to carry on business in the financial technology and intellectual property licensing sectors. Ask for a founders' subscription agreement that records your acquisition of common shares at nominal value.

|----------------|------------------------------------------|--------------|-----------------|
| **$1,500–$3,000** | **$200** | **10–15 business days** | **Technology startup lawyer** |

### 0.2 Transfer the Patent to the Company

Once the company number is issued by Corporations Canada, your patent lawyer files an Assignment of Patent Application with the USPTO and CIPO simultaneously, transferring the provisional application from you as an individual to Bridgepoint Intelligence Inc. This must happen before the company has any commercial conversations in which it represents itself as the patent owner — making a representation you know to be false creates legal exposure you do not want.

The assignment must be structured as a tax-deferred rollover under Section 85 of the Income Tax Act. This provision allows you to transfer intellectual property to a corporation you control without triggering a taxable disposition at the moment of transfer. Without this election, the Canada Revenue Agency may treat the transfer as a sale at fair market value and assess income tax on a gain that does not correspond to any cash you have actually received. Your startup lawyer and your accountant need to coordinate on this — the Section 85 Rollover Briefing Note in Folder 02 of this document set contains the full analysis and the list of questions to put to your accountant.

> **⚠️ Critical sequencing note:**
>
> The patent assignment filing with the USPTO and CIPO must happen within 30 days of incorporation and before any commercial pitch, grant application, or investor conversation in which the company claims to own the patent. The assignment recording takes 4–6 weeks at the USPTO. Your lawyer should file immediately after the company number is issued — do not wait for any other step.

## PHASE 1 — PFD + 1 TO 12 MONTHS
### IP Security
*Convert the provisional into a fortress before the clock expires*

**Two hard deadlines govern everything in this phase**

The provisional patent application bought you twelve months from the PFD. Every action in Phase 1 is constrained by two hard legal deadlines: the utility application must be filed by PFD + 12 months, and the PCT application — which locks in international protection — must be filed by PFD + 18 months. **These deadlines are not negotiable and not extendable. Missing either one is permanent and irreversible.**

### 1.1 Commission a Professional Prior Art Search

A professional search conducted by a registered patent search firm is materially different from a self-conducted search and serves a different purpose. The professional search is designed to find everything that could be used against you in prosecution, so your attorney can draft the utility claims with full knowledge of the landscape rather than being surprised by examiner rejections that were foreseeable.

The search should cover CPC classification codes G06Q20 (payment protocols), G06Q40 (finance and insurance), G06N20 (machine learning), and H04L67 (network-based applications). The assignee search must include SWIFT, Finastra, FIS Global, Bottomline Technologies, JPMorgan Technology, Citibank, Deutsche Bank Technology, and Temenos. Request a written opinion on the novelty of the five-element integrated system — that opinion becomes part of your prosecution record.

| **Professional search cost** | **Recommended firms** | **Completion target** | **Output used in** |
|-----------------------------|----------------------|----------------------|-------------------|
| **$8,000–$15,000** | **Dennemeyer, Clarivate Analytics** | **PFD + 2–3 months** | **Utility application claim drafting** |

### 1.2 File the Full Utility Application — USPTO

This is the most important legal filing in the portfolio. The utility application converts the provisional into a fully examined patent with a complete claim set, a prosecution record, and eventual issuance as an enforceable right. Every element of this filing must be done by a qualified patent attorney with specific experience in financial technology and machine learning patents.

The claim set from the **v5.1 provisional specification** — five independent claims and thirteen dependent claims (D1–D13) — constitutes the starting negotiating position with the examiner. The attorney's job during prosecution is to argue for the broadest possible interpretation of each claim element at every stage, and to resist narrowing amendments wherever a legal argument for broader scope is available. Every amendment made during prosecution becomes prosecution history that can be used to limit claim scope in future litigation.

> **📋 Instruction to give your patent attorney at the first briefing:**
>
> The foundational IP strategy is a 15-patent continuation family running to approximately 32 years after the PFD. The utility application is the anchor of that family. Every prosecution decision should be made with the continuation strategy in mind — specifically, claim elements that are narrowed during prosecution of P2 cannot be recaptured in continuation claims. The attorney should flag any proposed amendment that would limit: (1) the triggering mechanism (real-time payment status event versus forward-looking aggregate cash flow prediction), (2) the tiered PD framework, or (3) the UETR-based auto-repayment loop, because those three elements are the foundation of every subsequent continuation.
>
> Additionally: Dependent Claim D13 covering the adversarial camt.056 cancellation detection mechanism must be included in the utility application and must not be surrendered during prosecution without explicit instruction from Bridgepoint Intelligence. This claim element is the basis for P3 continuation claims and for the adversarial cancellation extension described in the Future Technology Disclosure.

### 1.3 File the PCT Application — International Coverage

The Patent Cooperation Treaty application locks in patent protection across the US, Canada, EU, Singapore, and UAE simultaneously, using the PFD priority date. Without the PCT filing, you would need to file separately in each jurisdiction as a national phase entry, which costs three to five times as much and leaves gaps in coverage during the filing period. The PCT buys you thirty months from the priority date to enter national phase in each jurisdiction.

> **🌍 Jurisdiction priority rationale:**
>
> **United States (USPTO):** primary licensing market — JPMorgan, Citi, FIS, and the largest payment technology ecosystem.
>
> **Canada (CIPO):** incorporation jurisdiction — RBC, Scotiabank, TD are first commercial targets.
>
> **European Patent Office (EPO via PCT):** Standard Chartered, HSBC, Deutsche Bank, ING, BNP Paribas.
>
> **Singapore (IPOS):** hub for Asian payment corridors where failure rates are highest; DBS headquarters; MAS Project Nexus CBDC initiative makes this the primary P6 jurisdiction.
>
> **UAE (MOHAP):** Gulf corridor — AED/INR and AED/PKR corridors have among the highest cross-border failure rates globally.

### 1.4 File P3 Continuation — Multi-Party Architecture and Adversarial Cancellation

The first continuation application should be filed before the utility application (P2) issues — which means filing it approximately PFD + 24 to 30 months, during the prosecution window. P3 covers two distinct and equally important design-around vectors.

First, the multi-party distributed architecture: implementations where the three system components are operated by different legal entities — a payment network operator providing monitoring, a fintech platform providing ML inference, and a bank providing execution and funding. Without this coverage, a competitor could argue under the *Akamai Technologies* doctrine that no single entity 'performs' the method of Claim 1 because the components are distributed across a commercial arrangement.

Second, the adversarial cancellation detection continuation: the camt.056 dual-stream monitoring architecture, the ML intent classifier distinguishing error-correction from adversarial cancellations, and the security interest preservation workflow described in Dependent Claim D13 and Future Technology Disclosure Extension G. These elements require a dedicated continuation claim set that goes beyond what can be accommodated in D13 of the utility application.

### Credibility Building
*Runs in parallel with Phase 1*

**Make the 90-second lookup compelling before any commercial meeting**

Banks and payment technology companies receive unsolicited approaches from hundreds of technology vendors every month. The vast majority are ignored before any human reads them. The filter applied in the first 90 seconds of evaluating an approach is credibility: does this entity look like something worth our time? Phase 2 constructs every credibility signal that makes the answer yes — the academic paper, the advisor network, and the professional web presence — before a single commercial outreach message is sent.

### 2.1 Post the Academic Paper to SSRN

The paper — *Real-Time Payment Failure Detection and Automated Liquidity Bridging in Cross-Border Payment Networks* (Document 7 of this document set, **v2.1**) — is already written. The immediate next step is to post it as a working paper on the Social Science Research Network (SSRN). SSRN registration is free. The paper is publicly timestamped within 48 hours of submission. This creates a dated public record of the intellectual contribution that predates any formal journal review timeline and is immediately searchable.

> **📄 SSRN timing — important:**
>
> **Do not post to SSRN before the utility patent is filed (PFD + 12 months).** Posting the paper before the utility application creates a public disclosure that may constitute prior art in some jurisdictions and could affect prosecution. Post to SSRN approximately one month after the utility filing is confirmed — approximately PFD + 13 months.
>
> After posting: email the paper directly to three or four researchers whose work you cite — specifically the authors of the most recent payment infrastructure papers in your reference list. ## PHASE 3 — PFD + 2 TO 6 MONTHS
### Non-Dilutive Funding
*Runs in parallel with Phases 1 and 2*

**Get government money before you need investor money**

The Canadian government provides more non-dilutive funding to technology innovation than almost any other country per capita. Most inventors never access it because they do not know it exists, file too late, or file incorrectly. Phase 3 is about capturing every dollar of government funding available to Bridgepoint Intelligence before any conversation with an equity investor begins — because every dollar of non-dilutive funding you receive is a dollar of equity you never have to sell.

### 3.1 NRC IRAP Application

The National Research Council's Industrial Research Assistance Programme is the single most important Canadian government programme for early-stage technology companies. IRAP provides non-repayable financial contributions of up to $500,000 for qualifying technical development work. The programme is specifically designed for companies at exactly your stage: post-concept, pre-revenue, with demonstrated technical innovation.

The application process begins with contacting an NRC Industrial Technology Advisor — an ITA — assigned to your region. In British Columbia, the Vancouver IRAP office serves the Lower Mainland. The ITA's job is to assess whether your project qualifies and to guide you through the application. Your patent, your published paper, and your working demo address all three assessment criteria — technical merit, capacity to execute, and commercial potential — more completely than most IRAP applicants can.

> **📞 Your first call — NRC IRAP Vancouver Office:**
>
> Call (604) 666-6000 and ask to speak with the Industrial Technology Advisor for your area. Describe Bridgepoint Intelligence as an early-stage fintech company developing patented machine learning infrastructure for cross-border payment failure detection and automated liquidity provisioning. Have the SSRN paper and the provisional patent number ready to share. The ITA will assess your eligibility in the first conversation and advise on the application timeline — which typically runs 6 to 12 weeks from first contact to funding decision.
>
> **Contact NRC IRAP no later than PFD + 3 months.** The programme is oversubscribed. Late applicants join a waitlist. Missing the annual intake cycle means waiting 12 months for the next one.

| **IRAP maximum contribution** | **Typical first-time range** | **Application to decision** | **Repayment required** |
|------------------------------|-----------------------------|-----------------------------|----------------------|
| **Up to $500,000** | **$50,000–$150,000** | **6–12 weeks** | **None — non-repayable** |

### 3.2 SR&ED Tax Credit Filing

The Scientific Research and Experimental Development programme allows Canadian-controlled private corporations to recover 35% to 65% of qualifying R&D expenditures through refundable tax credits. For a company spending $200,000 per year on qualifying development work, SR&ED can return $70,000 to $130,000 annually in refundable credits.

Qualifying SR&ED work specifically means work involving technological uncertainty, systematic investigation, and the generation of new technical knowledge. The development of the ML failure prediction model, the tiered PD framework calibration methodology, and the camt.056 adversarial event detection mechanism all qualify clearly. A qualified SR&ED consultant should review your expenditures quarterly and flag which activities and costs to document for the annual claim.

> **💡 SR&ED documentation practice — start on Day 1:**
>
> Create a project log from the moment of incorporation. Every meeting where you discuss a technical problem and how you are trying to solve it, every failed experiment, every hypothesis you test — document it with a date, a description of the technical uncertainty you were addressing, and the outcome. This contemporaneous documentation is the foundation of a defensible SR&ED claim. Trying to reconstruct it retroactively at tax filing time produces a weaker claim and increases audit risk.

## PHASE 4 — PFD + 6 TO 14 MONTHS
### First Commercial Conversations
*Credibility meets commercial intent*

**Sell the demonstration, not the patent**

Phase 4 is where credibility built in Phase 2 meets commercial intent. The goal is not to close a licensing deal in this phase — it is to qualify which institutions are genuinely interested and to advance two or three conversations to the point where a letter of intent or pilot agreement is achievable in Phase 5. The discipline of this phase is patience combined with strategic sequencing: approaching targets in the right order, with the right framing, through the right channels.

### 4.1 Target Sequencing — Who to Approach and in What Order

| **Priority** | **Institution Type** | **Why First** | **Expected Timeline to LOI** | **Entry Point** |
|-------------|---------------------|--------------|------------------------------|----------------|
| 1st | Payment technology companies (Finastra, FIS, Temenos, Nuvei) | Software companies move faster than banks. Product managers whose KPIs depend on shipping differentiating features. A licensing deal embeds your system in their platform and distributes it to hundreds of banks simultaneously. | 3–6 months | Chief Product Officer or Head of Payment Products via advisor introduction |
| 2nd | Ambitious tier-2 banks (Scotiabank, DBS, Standard Chartered, ING) | Large enough to have genuine cross-border infrastructure, not so large that they have internal engineering capacity to attempt building a competing system. Competitive anxiety is highest here. | 6–12 months | Head of Transaction Banking Product or Head of Cash Management Innovation via advisor introduction |
| 3rd | Canadian banks (RBC Ventures, TD Lab, Scotiabank Digital Factory) | Home jurisdiction, formal fintech partnership programmes, institutional credibility once you have a Canadian bank as reference customer. Target the innovation arms, not core banking procurement. | 4–8 months (via formal programme) | Formal application to fintech partnership programme, supported by advisor introduction to programme lead |

### 4.2 The Outreach Message — What to Say and What Never to Say

> **What the first outreach message must do:**
>
> Name the specific commercial problem their institution faces — not the generic industry problem, but the specific competitive situation they are in. Reference something concrete about their business that demonstrates you have done real research.
>
> State what the system does in one sentence, in business language: *'A system that automatically bridges cash flow gaps caused by delayed cross-border payments and collects repayment the moment the original payment settles — without human intervention.'* Never lead with ISO 20022, gradient boosting, or any technical term.
>
> Request a 20-minute demonstration call, not a meeting, not a pitch, not a proposal. A demonstration is lower commitment and positions you as showing them something rather than asking them for something.
>
> Include one credibility signal in the signature — the patent number, the SSRN paper link, or the IRAP grant if approved. One signal is more credible than three.

> **🚫 What to never say in a first outreach:**
>
> Never mention royalties, licensing fees, or equity in the first message. Never use the word 'revolutionary' or 'disruptive.' Never send a deck as an attachment — it signals that you are a vendor rather than a peer. Never mention how many other institutions you are talking to, even if it is true — save competitive urgency for a later stage when it carries more weight because the relationship is already established.

### 4.3 The Demonstration Meeting — Running It Correctly

The live demo is your most powerful commercial instrument. Every output is real. Every latency number is measured. Every patent claim label is legally grounded. A bank's product team has never seen anything like it, and the combination of technical rigour and commercial clarity is disorienting in the best possible way — they expected a slide deck and they are watching a live system make a lending decision at median latency under 100 milliseconds.

Run the demo against a scenario specific to the institution you are meeting. If you are meeting Scotiabank, use a USD/MXN or USD/COP payment scenario. If you are meeting Standard Chartered, use an emerging market corridor — USD/NGN or USD/KES — where correspondent banking chains are longest and failure rates are highest. The specificity signals that you understand their business, not just the general problem.

End every demonstration meeting with two specific asks and nothing else. First, ask for permission to send a one-page technical summary and a copy of the paper. Second, ask who else inside the institution would find this relevant — a referral to a second person transforms a cold single-thread conversation into a warm multi-thread relationship. Do not propose a term sheet. Do not mention price. Let the question *'what would it take to access this?'* come from them.

## PHASE 5 — PFD + 12 TO 24 MONTHS
### Pilot Deployment
*The single most important commercial event in the company's lifecycle*

**Live data is worth more than any conversation**

The pilot deployment transforms Bridgepoint Intelligence from a holder of promising IP into a provider of a system with demonstrated real-world performance. Every conversation after the pilot is qualitatively different from every conversation before it.

### 5.1 Pilot Structure — What to Offer and What to Protect

> **Pilot Term Sheet — Recommended Structure:**
>
> **Duration:** 90 days from the date live payment data begins flowing.
>
> **Payment corridors:** three to five corridors identified jointly with the partner, chosen to represent the highest failure rate routes in their portfolio.
>
> **Volume cap:** up to 10,000 payment events per day monitored, with bridge loan offers capped at a total advance pool of $5 million to limit the partner's financial exposure during evaluation.
>
> **Success metric:** AUC ≥ 0.700 and recall ≥ 0.780 on the partner's live payment data. Slightly below the 0.739 and 0.810 achieved on the development dataset, appropriate since live data from a new institution will have distribution characteristics the model has not seen.
>
> **IP ownership — non-negotiable:** all improvements, optimisations, and derivative innovations developed during or following the pilot remain the exclusive property of Bridgepoint Intelligence Inc. The partner receives no rights to continuation patents, model improvements, or any element of the system beyond the evaluation access granted under this agreement.
>
> **Path to full licence:** the agreement should include a right of first negotiation for a full licence within 60 days of pilot completion if performance metrics are met.

The right pilot pricing structure is a nominal pilot fee — $25,000 to $50,000 — described as covering integration support and technical consultation, with a credit against the first year of the full licence fee if the partner proceeds. This ensures the pilot is a commercial transaction rather than a favour.

### 5.2 Technical Integration — What Connecting to a Bank's Systems Actually Means

The integration typically happens through one of three pathways. The sandbox pathway — the bank provides a test feed of anonymised historical payment events in ISO 20022 format — bypasses most compliance review because no real customer data is involved. The read-only API pathway provides live event data without write access. The full integration pathway enables live bridge loan disbursement through the bank's systems. Start with the sandbox, demonstrate performance, and let the partner's interest drive the integration deeper.

> **🔒 Compliance and data governance requirements to address proactively:**
>
> Before any live data integration, Bridgepoint Intelligence will need: a completed vendor security assessment questionnaire (each bank has their own, typically 200–400 questions covering data handling, encryption, access controls, incident response, and business continuity); cyber liability insurance with limits of at least $5M CAD; a data processing agreement compliant with PIPEDA in Canada and GDPR if the bank has EU operations; and a clear data residency position specifying that payment event data is processed in Canada and not transmitted to third-party infrastructure without the bank's consent.

### First Licensing Deal
*Everything in Phases 0–5 has been preparation for this phase*

**Convert the pilot into recurring revenue**

### 6.1 The Deal Structure That Protects Long-Term Value

> **Recommended Licence Structure — First Deal:**
>
> **Exclusivity:** time-limited, geography- or market-segment-specific. Offer 18 to 24 months of exclusivity in a defined region (e.g., Canadian dollar payment corridors) or a defined segment (e.g., mid-market cross-border trade finance). This gives the partner the competitive advantage they need to justify the deal internally while preserving your ability to licence globally after the exclusivity window expires.
>
> **Annual royalty:** a recurring fee tied to transaction volume — for example, 2 basis points per dollar of payment volume monitored, with a minimum annual commitment of $2M to $5M. Volume-based pricing means your revenue grows automatically as the bank's payment volumes grow, without renegotiation.
>
> **Most-favoured-nation clause:** offer this voluntarily. It guarantees the partner that you will never license the same rights to a competitor on more favourable terms. Banks love this clause and offering it proactively builds significant goodwill.
>
> **IP ownership carve-out — the non-negotiable:** the licence agreement must explicitly state that all continuation patents, model improvements, calibration data, and derivative innovations remain the exclusive property of Bridgepoint Intelligence Inc. The partner receives a licence to use the current system. They acquire no rights, ownership interest, or co-inventorship claim over any future IP. This clause will be pushed back on by the bank's legal team. Do not concede it under any circumstances.

### 6.2 The Seed Raise — If and When

The seed raise is conditional, not inevitable. If NRC IRAP funding covers the operational costs through the pilot phase, and if the pilot generates the reference customer that opens the first licensing conversation, it is entirely possible to reach a signed licensing agreement without raising any external equity — the most favourable possible outcome.

The seed raise becomes necessary if: the IRAP application is delayed or underfunded, the pilot requires technical integration resources that exceed grant funding, or the business development effort requires a dedicated hire that grant funding does not cover. If a raise is required, the right time is after the pilot LOI is signed but before the full licence is executed — because an LOI from a recognisable financial institution transforms the investment narrative from 'promising technology' to 'technology with a named customer about to pay for it.'

The target raise is $1 million to $2 million at a post-money valuation of $8 million to $15 million. Target investors: QED Investors, Portage Ventures (Canada), Ribbit Capital, or BDC Ventures. The pitch narrative is: *'We have a patent-protected system with a signed pilot agreement at a major financial institution, and we need capital to close the full licence and file four continuation patents over the next 24 months.'*
