## 1. The Portfolio Philosophy — Why One Patent Is Never Enough

A single patent is a door with one lock. A well-architected patent family is a fortress with fifteen walls, each one independently unbreachable, each one covering a different angle of attack. The goal of this portfolio strategy is not merely to protect the original invention — it is to build a network of overlapping, mutually reinforcing intellectual property rights that makes it economically irrational for any competitor to attempt to build a competing system without either licensing from Bridgepoint Intelligence or spending more on litigation than the market opportunity is worth.

The strategy works on four dimensions simultaneously. Temporal depth: by filing continuation applications over a twelve-year period, the last patent in the family does not expire until approximately 32 years after the foundational filing. Technical breadth: each continuation covers a different aspect of the system, so that a competitor who designs around one patent finds themselves infringing another. Market coverage: as the underlying payments market evolves — from SWIFT to CBDC to AI-managed treasury — each new wave of evolution is anticipated and claimed before it becomes obvious to the field. Trade secret synergy: the patent portfolio makes the trade secrets more valuable, and the trade secrets make the patents more valuable, because together they create a system that is simultaneously legally protected and practically impossible to replicate without years of proprietary deployment data.

### 1.1 The Continuation Mechanism — How One Filing Date Covers Thirty-Two Years

The most important concept in this entire document is how continuation applications work, because it is the mechanism that allows a single provisional priority date to underpin a portfolio whose last expiry is approximately 32 years later. When you file a patent application, you are required to disclose the full scope of your invention. Everything described in that disclosure is your intellectual property from the filing date forward. But you are not required to claim everything you disclose in a single application. You can file additional applications — continuation applications — at any time before the original patent issues, and each continuation claims the benefit of the original priority date but introduces entirely new claims covering different aspects of the disclosed invention. Each continuation application then has its own independent 20-year term measured from its own filing date.

The practical implication is profound. A continuation filed approximately six years after the foundational filing, covering the CBDC extension, expires 26 years after foundational filing. A continuation filed approximately ten years after foundational filing, covering the AI Treasury Agent, expires 30 years after foundational filing. A continuation filed approximately twelve years after foundational filing, covering the tokenised receivable pool architecture, expires 32 years after foundational filing. These continuations are not vulnerable to prior art published after the foundational filing date, because their effective priority date is the date of the original provisional — a date at which none of those technologies existed in commerce.

The only requirement that makes this possible is that the extension technology must be described in sufficient technical detail in the original disclosure package filed or incorporated at the foundational filing. This is why the Future Technology Disclosure v2.1 — document 02 of this portfolio — is not optional. It is the legal foundation on which the entire 32-year portfolio rests. If an extension is not described there before the provisional is filed, it cannot claim the foundational priority date. It must be filed as a new application with a later priority date, vulnerable to everything published in the intervening years.

This document should not overstate certainty on that point. Some later-filed extensions may ultimately require continuation-in-part treatment rather than pure continuations if prosecution counsel concludes that the foundational provisional does not provide sufficient written-description support for the full extension claim set. The strategic rule is therefore: file as continuations where the parent disclosure supports it; use CIP treatment promptly where genuinely new matter is unavoidable; and make that determination extension by extension with counsel before P2 is finalised.

### 1.2 The Five-Element Integration — Why the Combination Is the Invention

The prior art search confirmed that each of the five individual elements of the foundational system has antecedents in the prior art — real-time payment monitoring exists, ML classification of financial events exists, automated liquidity triggering exists, PD-from-observables exists, and automated repayment collection exists in analogous structures. What does not exist, in any single prior art reference or any obvious combination of references, is the integrated five-element system operating on live cross-border payment network telemetry with the specific triggering mechanism, pricing architecture, and settlement-confirmation repayment loop described in the foundational specification.

This means the portfolio's strength is not in any single claim element — it is in the integration. A competitor who successfully designs around Claim 1's trigger mechanism still needs a pricing model. If they use a PD-based pricing model without an assumed credit spread, they are in the scope of Claims 2 and 3's elements. If they add automated repayment, they are in the scope of Claim 5. If they distribute the system across multiple entities, they are in the scope of P3. If they add pre-emptive capability, they are in the scope of P4. There is no path through the portfolio that does not require a licensing conversation.

### 2.1 Core Patents (P1–P5) — File Within 36 Months of Provisional

#### P1 / P2: The Foundational Patent — The Anchor of Everything

P1 is the provisional application. Its sole purpose is to establish the priority date for the entire portfolio. Every continuation, every international filing, every licensing negotiation, every enforcement action ultimately traces its authority back to this single filing date. The quality of the provisional specification determines the quality of everything that follows. The current provisional specification — paired with Future Technology Disclosure v2.1 and reviewed by counsel for incorporation and written-description sufficiency — is the foundational disclosure package.

P2 is the full utility application. It must be filed within 12 months of the provisional — this is an absolute legal deadline with no exceptions and no extensions. P2 converts the provisional into a fully examined patent with a complete claim set, a prosecution record, and eventual issuance as an enforceable right. Every element of this filing must be done by a qualified patent attorney with specific experience in financial technology and machine learning patents.

The claim set for P2 is the five independent claims and thirteen dependent claims described in the current provisional specification:

> **Claim 1:** Core Method Claim — real-time payment failure detection → ML scoring → risk-adjusted pricing → automated liquidity offer → settlement-confirmation repayment. Refined in the current prosecution-ready draft with precise trigger-distinction language that preserves the doctrine of equivalents while maintaining the Bottomline US11532040B2 distinction.
>
> **Claim 2:** System Claim — six-component architecture. Refined in the current prosecution-ready draft with trigger-distinction element (i-a) in the monitoring component, closing the Bottomline §103 vector at the system claim level.
>
> **Claim 3:** Instrument-Agnostic Liquidity Method — covers any financial instrument structure including bridge loans, receivable purchases, guarantees, and revolving credit draws. Refined in the current prosecution-ready draft with trigger-restriction preamble and a new opening step tying the method to a real-time payment failure signal.
>
> **Claim 4:** Pre-Emptive Liquidity — forward-looking individual-payment-level hazard model aggregated into portfolio gap distribution. The value of P2 Claim 4 is broad platform coverage for specific anticipated payment events; the value of P4 is narrower follow-on protection for named operational embodiments such as Payment Expectation Graph maintenance, Bayesian updating from observed outcomes, quantile-based facility calibration, and facility drawdown logic.
>
> **Claim 5:** Settlement-Confirmation Auto-Repayment Loop — the net at the bottom of every possible design-around. Any competitor implementing automated repayment collection using payment network confirmation data infringes this claim regardless of how they have engineered around Claims 1–4.

The thirteen dependent claims D1 through D13 provide specific embodiment coverage for ISO 20022 pacs.002 parsing, SWIFT gpi UETR tracking, F-beta threshold optimisation, three-tier PD estimation, bridge loan and receivable purchase instruments, median sub-100ms latency, embedded software implementation, SWIFT gpi auto-repayment, private-company PD methodology, and the adversarial camt.056 cancellation detection architecture.

> **THE MOST CRITICAL PROSECUTION INSTRUCTION:** Every prosecution decision on narrowing amendments to Claims 1–4 must be evaluated for its effect on continuation claim scope. Elements narrowed during prosecution of P2 cannot be recaptured in continuation claims. The attorney should flag any proposed amendment that would limit the triggering mechanism, the PD framework, the repayment loop, or the multi-party architecture — those four elements are the foundation on which P3 through P8 depend. Resist narrowing at every stage. Argue for broad interpretation. File continuations before conceding anything that matters.

#### P3: Multi-Party Architecture, Embedded Implementation, and Adversarial Cancellation

P3 is the most strategically important continuation in the family for the first decade of commercial deployment. It covers two distinct novel elements that v1.0 of this architecture treated as one: the multi-party distributed architecture described in FTD Extension F, and the adversarial camt.056 cancellation detection mechanism described in FTD Extension G and captured in Dependent Claim D13 of P2.

The multi-party element addresses the single most dangerous design-around vector against the foundational claims. Under the *Akamai Technologies, Inc. v. Limelight Networks, Inc.* (Fed. Cir. 2015) standard, a competitor can avoid direct infringement of a method claim by distributing the steps of the method across three separate legal entities — a monitoring operator, an ML inference provider, and a lending bank — such that no single entity performs the complete method. Without P3, a commercially structured implementation of the system using this three-entity arrangement cannot be reached by P2's method claims.

P3 closes this gap in three ways. It claims the three-entity arrangement affirmatively as a specific embodiment. It drafts the claim elements to capture the joint enterprise relationship between the entities using the *Akamai* standard's own doctrinal language. And it includes the API data flow between entities as a specific technical claim element, making the distributed arrangement itself patentable subject matter rather than an infringing workaround.

The adversarial cancellation element of P3 builds on Dependent Claim D13 of P2 to file broader independent continuation claims covering the dual-stream monitoring architecture and the security interest preservation workflow as standalone patentable subject matter. D13 depends on Claim 5 and therefore inherits P2's priority; P3's broader independent claims expand the coverage to protect the cancellation detection system independently of the auto-repayment loop it was designed to protect.

> **P3 FILING URGENCY:** P3 must be filed before P2 issues AND before Bridgepoint's first commercial deployment is publicly announced. Once the commercial arrangement involving a bank partner is publicly known, a competitor can observe the distributed structure and pre-date P3's filing with their own disclosure. The filing window is approximately Year 2 to Year 3 from provisional. It cannot be delayed without forfeiting coverage of the most commercially relevant implementation structure.

#### P4: Pre-Emptive Liquidity Portfolio Management

P4 covers the pre-emptive forward-looking architecture described in Independent Claim 4 of P2 and in FTD Extension A, but with a more detailed embodiment-level claim set than the foundational independent claim. Specifically, P4 should not merely restate P2 Claim 4. Its proper scope is the Payment Expectation Graph data structure, Bayesian updating of receipt-date distributions from observed payment outcomes, quantile-based facility calibration logic, the time-conditional hazard model as a standalone subcomponent, and the automatic facility drawdown mechanism as individually protectable elements that go beyond the broader platform claim in P2.

Pre-emptive liquidity management is the natural commercial evolution that every major bank's treasury technology team will eventually build. The competitive threat is not from startups — it is from the internal R&D programmes of the exact institutions Bridgepoint is licensing to. Filing P4 approximately three years after the foundational provisional ensures that when JPMorgan or HSBC concludes that pre-emptive payment bridging is valuable and assigns their technology team to build it, they find a continuation patent with a filing date that predates their development programme by years. P4 is the single most time-sensitive continuation in the family.

#### P5: Supply Chain Cascade Detection and Prevention

P5 covers the network topology model and cascade risk propagation algorithm described in FTD Extension B. The core claim element — computing cascade propagation probabilities for downstream supply chain nodes upon upstream payment failure, and identifying the minimum-cost coordinated bridge intervention — is a novel application of graph theory and combinatorial optimisation to payment network risk management with no identified prior art. P5 should also expressly reserve the pre-emptive cascade prevention mode disclosed in Extension B.4, where forward failure probabilities are propagated through the network before any first payment has actually failed.

P5 should be filed when the first supply chain monitoring deployment data is available, because the specific graph construction methodology and financial dependency scoring algorithm will be most defensible when grounded in observed deployment data rather than theoretical descriptions alone. Target: approximately four years after the provisional filing.

### 2.2 Extension Patents (P6–P10) — File as Technology Matures

#### P6: CBDC Settlement Failure Detection and Bridging

Central bank digital currencies represent the most significant structural change in payment infrastructure since the creation of SWIFT. The technical failure modes of CBDC settlement are fundamentally different from correspondent banking failures: instead of ISO 20022 rejection reason codes, you have smart contract execution failures, interoperability protocol errors between different CBDC networks, and cryptographic validation failures. These failure types require a CBDC-specific feature extraction layer and a normalisation architecture that translates platform-specific event schemas into the foundational system's failure classification taxonomy.

File P6 when at least two major international CBDC interoperability standards are published — estimated approximately five to six years after the foundational filing based on current G20 CBDC development timelines. Filing before standards are published risks writing claims against a moving technical target; filing more than 12 months after standards publication risks that the architectural approach becomes obvious from the standards themselves.

#### P7: Tokenised Receivable Liquidity Pool Architecture

P7 covers the transformation of bridge loans from a bilateral credit product into a capital markets product through real-time competitive Dutch auction of tokenised payment receivables. The key novel elements are: the cryptographic binding of the digital receivable token to the live UETR tracking data, enabling continuous status verification by token holders; the Dutch auction mechanism initialised at the CVA-derived fair value; and the automatic token redemption upon UETR settlement confirmation. These three elements in combination have no identified prior art and represent a genuine capital markets innovation.

P7 should be filed when institutional distributed ledger infrastructure is sufficiently standardised to make the tokenisation layer commercially deployable — estimated approximately six years after the foundational filing. Filing earlier would allow competitors to observe the architecture from the published patent and build to it before the infrastructure exists to support it.

#### P8: AI-Powered Autonomous Treasury Management Agent

P8 covers the configurable decision authority framework — the three-tier autonomous/escalation/approval structure — as a novel architecture for human-AI collaborative decision-making in financial operations. The probability-adjusted FX hedging formula — optimal hedge ratio equals one minus payment failure probability times standard hedge ratio — is a specific quantitative method that integrates payment network risk into currency exposure management in a way that no existing treasury system implements. These are independently patentable elements that go well beyond the autonomous treasury concept.

#### P9: Cross-Network Interoperability Bridging

P9 addresses the bridging challenge that will emerge as multiple payment rails — SWIFT gpi, FedNow, CIPS, various CBDC networks — operate in parallel with different settlement finalities, message formats, and failure characteristics. The core claim covers a monitoring system that tracks a single payment transaction across multiple rail hand-offs and detects failure events that occur at the transition between rails rather than within any single rail. This inter-rail failure mode is currently unclaimed and will become increasingly commercially significant as payment fragmentation increases.

#### P10: Systemic Risk Monitoring and Regulatory Reporting Layer

P10 covers the application of the portfolio-level payment failure data accumulated through commercial operation to real-time systemic risk monitoring. The system aggregates failure rates, cascade propagation events, and working capital gap data across all monitored entities to produce corridor-level and network-level systemic risk metrics that can be provided to regulators via API feeds. This creates a regulatory data product that financial regulators — the BIS, OSFI, the FCA, the ECB — cannot currently produce without Bridgepoint's proprietary deployment data.

### 2.3 Long-Horizon Patents (P11–P15) — The Coverage Layer

Patents P11 through P15 represent the long-horizon coverage layer — the claims that will be active when today's technology has been superseded and the underlying market has grown to $200 trillion or more in annual cross-border B2B payment volume.[^1] By describing these extensions in technical detail in Future Technology Disclosure v2.1 before the provisional is filed, we establish that they were conceived at the foundational priority date, giving them priority over anyone who independently develops and publishes the same concepts in the intervening years.

[^1]: The $200T+ long-horizon figure represents an internal projection derived from the FXC Intelligence 2024 baseline of $31.7T at the SWIFT-documented 7% annual CAGR compounded approximately 28 years. It is not a published forecast and should not be cited externally as such.

#### P11: SME Embedded Payments and Liquidity (B2B2X)

P11 covers the distribution of automated payment bridging through marketplace platforms and B2B software providers, where the marketplace operator is the commercial counterparty and the underlying SME businesses are the end beneficiaries. The novel claim element is the B2B2X architecture in which the marketplace's API layer sits between the Bridgepoint system and the SME, with the marketplace operator assuming the commercial and compliance interface obligations and Bridgepoint providing the technology and liquidity infrastructure behind the API. This structure is not covered by P3's multi-party architecture, which addresses the three-entity arrangement at the financial institution level rather than the marketplace distribution level.

#### P12: ML Model Continuous Learning and Federated Training

P12 covers the privacy-preserving federated learning architecture that enables the failure prediction model to improve from deployment data across multiple licensed institutions without any institution sharing raw payment data with Bridgepoint or with each other. The core claim element is the use of differential privacy mechanisms applied to locally computed model gradient updates, which are aggregated centrally without exposing the underlying data. P12 should also claim the method of constructing and maintaining the BIC-pair performance database and related calibration tables from live payment telemetry: ingesting payment status outcomes, updating empirical failure-rate distributions by sender-receiver pair and time window, and refreshing model calibration inputs as each new outcome is observed. As deployment across multiple institutions creates a multi-institution training and calibration dataset that no single institution possesses, the federated model and performance database become progressively more accurate — a compounding competitive advantage protected by P12.

#### P13: Carbon-Aware Payment Routing and Sustainability Scoring

P13 anticipates a regulatory trend that is already legislatively visible. The EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive, Canada's proposed mandatory climate disclosure framework, and similar regulatory developments are creating pressure for financial institutions to track and disclose the environmental footprint of their financial activities including payment routing. P13 covers a system that assigns carbon intensity scores to payment corridors based on the energy mix of the correspondent banking infrastructure in each routing path, and optimises payment routing to minimise environmental impact subject to cost and reliability constraints. Filing this continuation approximately ten years after the foundational provisional ensures coverage for a market that does not yet exist commercially but whose regulatory foundations are being laid today.

#### P14 and P15: AI-Native Intelligence and Quantum-Resistant Security

P14 covers the embedding of AI inference capabilities directly into payment network infrastructure — the architectural transition from AI as an overlay on payment systems to AI as a native layer of payment infrastructure itself. P15 covers the application of post-quantum cryptographic algorithms to the UETR-based settlement monitoring infrastructure, ensuring that the monitoring and repayment loop remains secure against quantum computing attacks on the elliptic curve cryptography currently used in SWIFT's tracking infrastructure. Both continuations are long-horizon filings whose commercial relevance will be determined by the pace of technological development in their respective areas.

## 6. Trade Secret Synergy — The Layer That Patents Cannot Provide

The patent portfolio and the trade secret layer are not substitutes — they are complements. The patent portfolio provides legal exclusivity: the right to exclude others from using the claimed methods regardless of how they obtained the knowledge. The trade secret layer provides practical exclusivity: the impossibility of replicating the system's performance without years of proprietary training data and calibration data, even if the technical architecture is fully known.

A competitor who reads every claim of every patent in the portfolio and attempts to build a competing system from scratch will produce a system that, in its first year of operation, performs materially worse than Bridgepoint's system in its fourth year of operation. Not because any patent blocked them from implementing the architecture — patents do not prevent implementation, they prevent commercial use without a licence. But because they lack the proprietary calibration data that makes the architecture perform at the level the architecture is capable of.

| **Trade Secret Asset** | **Patent Relationship** | **Compounding Value Over Time** |
|------------------------|------------------------|--------------------------------|
| **Calibrated ML model weights and feature importance rankings** | Patent discloses architecture and training methodology; weights are not disclosed and cannot be reverse-engineered from architecture alone | Model improves continuously with each payment outcome observed. After 4 years of deployment at 3 institutions, the model has seen orders of magnitude more training examples than any new entrant can produce. Performance gap widens annually. |
| **BIC-pair corridor performance database** | Patent discloses that corridor-specific rejection rates are used as features; the specific rates for thousands of BIC pairs are not disclosed | Database grows with each new banking partnership. After 5 years, contains performance data on tens of thousands of routing pairs that new entrants cannot obtain without equivalent deployment history. |
| **CVA parameter calibrations by sector and corridor** | Patent discloses LGD and friction adjustment framework; specific parameter values calibrated from live portfolio performance are not disclosed | Calibrations become more precise with each completed bridge loan cycle. After 3 years, parameters reflect actual realised LGD from a live portfolio — no academic estimate or theoretical calibration can match this. |
| **Adversarial cancellation classifier training dataset** | Patent (D13) discloses feature categories; the labelled dataset of camt.056 events classified as adversarial vs. error-correction is not disclosed and does not exist in any public source | Dataset grows with each detected cancellation event across all deployments. After 4 years, the classifier has seen and correctly classified hundreds of adversarial attempts. A new entrant begins with no training data at all. |

> **THE COMPOUNDING ADVANTAGE IN NUMBERS:** Year 1 of deployment: Bridgepoint model AUC 0.739 (validated on historical data). Year 3 of deployment: estimated AUC 0.780–0.800 from live training data at 2–3 institutions. Year 7 of deployment: estimated AUC 0.820–0.850 from federated training across 10+ institutions. A competitor entering at Year 7 with a cold-start model produces AUC ~0.700 — a performance gap of 0.12–0.15 AUC points. In the payment failure prediction context, that gap translates directly into missed bridge opportunities and incorrect credit pricing. The gap is not closable by any means other than years of live deployment data. It is the compound interest of operational history.
