## Tier D — Deep-dive

### 1. Market sizing methodology

TAM: thirty-one point six trillion dollars ($31.6T) in annual B2B cross-border payment volume — FXC Intelligence 2024, B2B value only. CAGR is five point nine percent (5.9%); seven percent (7%) is SWIFT message count growth, not value growth.

**Disrupted volume.** At four percent midpoint failure rate: one point two seven trillion dollars ($1.27T) annually. Supported by the one hundred eighteen point five billion dollar ($118.5B) global cost of failed payments (Accuity/LexisNexis 2021) and the LexisNexis fourteen percent first-attempt failure estimate under a broader definition.

**Addressable bridge volume.** Fifty percent of disrupted volume after exclusions — eight BLOCK-class investigatory hold codes permanently excluded; payments below minimum principal floors (one million five hundred thousand dollars ($1,500,000) Class A; seven hundred thousand dollars ($700K) Class B) excluded. Result: approximately six hundred thirty billion dollars ($630B). BPI captures thirty percent of licensee fee revenue in Phase 1.

**Per-bank SOM.** One Tier 1 bank processes three to five trillion dollars ($3T–$5T) in annual correspondent volume. At four percent failure rate and fifty percent bridgeable fraction, per-bank bridge principal runs to sixty to one hundred billion dollars annually. Revenue projections use seven-day average maturity, fifty-two capital cycles per year, eight hundred thousand to one million dollar ($800K–$1M) average principal, three hundred fifty to four hundred basis point average fee.

### 2. Competitive landscape

**JPMorgan Chase.** The closest analogue. US7089207B1 (2006) automates bridge lending for listed counterparties using observable equity market data. Three structural gaps: Tier 1 listed companies only (no private counterparty coverage); static pre-negotiated terms (no real-time risk pricing); no connection to ISO 20022 pacs.002 rejection telemetry. JPMorgan's Kinexys platform (formerly Onyx) handles tokenised repo and blockchain interbank settlement — a complementary but different problem from failure-triggered bridge lending.

**BNY Mellon.** Correspondent-banking incumbent; deep custody and clearing infrastructure; no public product addressing real-time failure classification or automated bridge offer generation.

**Wise.** Consumer and SMB FX transfers, typical transaction size two hundred to fifty thousand dollars ($200–$50K). Different rails, different segment, no competitive overlap with correspondent banking failure bridging.

**Ripple / RippleNet.** On-Demand Liquidity uses XRP for pre-funded corridor settlement — approximately thirty billion dollars ($30B) annualised versus one hundred fifty trillion dollars or more on SWIFT. Different mechanism (pre-funded liquidity vs. post-failure bridge) and different scale.

**Bottomline Technologies.** ML-based aggregate cash flow forecasting at portfolio level; taken private by Thoma Bravo in 2022 for approximately three point six billion dollars ($3.6B). Primary §103 obviousness risk for LIP patent claims — mitigated by the distinction between portfolio-level forecasting (Bottomline) and individual UETR-keyed payment-event bridging (LIP).

**Finastra.** Banking infrastructure provider to eight thousand plus financial institutions; FusionFabric.cloud is a candidate distribution channel for Phase 3, not a competitor in failure-bridging.

**The gap.** No incumbent builds a real-time classification layer on ISO 20022 pacs.002 rejection codes, conditions a bridge loan offer on that classification, and extends credit pricing to private counterparties via Damodaran industry-beta and Altman Z' thin-file models. That specific combination is unoccupied.

### 3. Regulatory tailwinds

**ISO 20022 migration.** SWIFT's coexistence period ran through November 2025. Structured pacs.002 rejection codes are now standardised across eleven thousand plus member institutions. The migration created the data environment that makes real-time failure classification tractable. This is the ISO 20022 migration window anchor: the window opened; LIP is the first system built on the structured data it produced.

**AMLD6 (EU).** Article 10 establishes criminal liability for legal persons in regulatory compliance failures. Banks must build investigatory hold discrimination infrastructure to avoid bridging compliance-held payments. LIP's hold-type discriminator — the BLOCK-class gate that permanently excludes the eight investigatory hold codes — is directly aligned with AMLD6 Article 10 obligations. This is a compliance infrastructure sell, not a positioning claim.

**DORA (EU).** Digital Operational Resilience Act. Relevant to Phase 2 and Phase 3 European bank onboarding — LIP's operational resilience documentation, audit trail architecture, and decision log retention at seven years are DORA-aligned.

**EU AI Act.** Article 14 human oversight requirements. LIP's C6 anomaly routing to PENDING_HUMAN_REVIEW (EPG-18) is a direct implementation of Article 14. Tier 2/3 European banks facing EU AI Act compliance pressure have an audit-ready implementation as part of the LIP license.

**OSFI E-23.** Canada's SR 11-7 equivalent, effective May 2027. LIP pre-generates compliant model validation reports covering out-of-time validation, data cards, and model cards. Canadian bank pilots gain a compliance artefact, not a compliance burden.

### 4. Regulatory headwinds

**AMLD7 (pending, EU).** The proposed seventh Anti-Money Laundering Directive may expand the scope of liability provisions and tighten hold-enforcement reclassification requirements. If investigatory hold definitions are widened, LIP's BLOCK-class exclusion list may need expansion — adding development and redeployment cost, and potentially narrowing the addressable bridge volume. The bear-case volume covers this scenario.

**Hold-enforcement reclassification risk.** LIP's Class B failure class covers systemic and processing delays — not investigatory holds, which are permanently blocked. A regulatory reclassification that moves a payment type from systemic delay into investigatory hold category would shrink the bridgeable Class B pool. This risk is structural rather than existential: the BLOCK-class gate was designed to expand, not shrink, the exclusion list as new hold types are identified. Any expansion reduces addressable volume; it does not create compliance exposure.

**Lending license (Phase 2/3).** Phase 1 licensing generates royalty income — BPI is not acting as a lender. Phase 2 and Phase 3 involve BPI capital deployment into bridge loans. At that point, OSFI engagement is required for Canadian operations. This is a known gating requirement factored into the Phase 2 timeline; it is not a surprise headwind.

### 6. Revenue model

Phase 1 is licensing revenue classified as royalty income. The originating bank funds one hundred percent of bridge loan capital. BPI earns a thirty percent share of the fee generated on each loan. The fee floor is three hundred basis points; every loan executed is capital-positive for BPI.

Fee tiers: five hundred basis points for loans below five hundred thousand dollars ($500K); four hundred basis points for the five hundred thousand to two million dollar ($500K–$2M) range; three hundred basis points at or above two million dollars ($2M) — the canonical fee floor. The live demo: a two-million eight-hundred ninety-thousand-dollar ($2,890,000) bridge priced at seven hundred six basis points, nine-day maturity, total fee five thousand and thirty-three dollars ($5,033), annualised yield seven point zero six percent (7.06%).

Phase 2 transitions to hybrid capital: BPI and an SPV contribute seventy percent of capital, the bank contributes thirty percent, and BPI earns a fifty-five percent fee share. Phase 3 is full MLO structure: BPI funds one hundred percent via the SPV, earns an eighty percent fee share, bank earns a twenty percent distribution premium. Income classification changes materially from Phase 1 (royalty) to Phase 2/3 (lending revenue) — a tax and legal distinction that has been reviewed.

Revenue projections. Conservative Year 3 (single Tier 1 bank, Phase 1): six million dollars ($6.0M). Base Year 3: twenty-three million dollars ($23.0M). Conservative Year 10 (fifteen banks): approximately two hundred forty-one million dollars ($241M). Base Year 10: approximately nine hundred twenty-one million dollars ($921M). Upside Year 10: approximately three point two billion dollars ($3.2B). Cumulative base case Years 3–10: approximately two point two billion dollars ($2.2B).

The thirty percent fee share in Phase 1 compounds as the licensee base grows. Each additional bank onboarded adds a revenue stream that does not require proportionally more BPI capital — the licensing model is the capital efficiency argument.
