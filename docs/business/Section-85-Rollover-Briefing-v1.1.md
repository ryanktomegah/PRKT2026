## 1. Why This Election Is Required

Bridgepoint Intelligence Inc. is being incorporated to commercialise the Liquidity Intelligence Platform technology developed personally by the founder. For the company to own the intellectual property — required for patent prosecution in the company's name, for licensing agreements, and for investor due diligence — the IP must be legally transferred from the founder to the company.

Without a Section 85 election, a transfer of property from an individual to a corporation is treated as a disposition at fair market value for tax purposes. Using a $300,000 FMV hypothetical as an example, the tax consequence depends on the capital gains inclusion rate applicable at the time of transfer — **which requires confirmation from the accountant before this election is structured** (see Question 7 below):

> **⚠️ INCLUSION RATE — CONFIRM BEFORE FILING:**
>
> **Scenario A — 1/2 inclusion rate (pre-June 2024 regime, or if the 2024 budget proposal was not enacted):**
> A $300,000 capital gain × 1/2 = $150,000 included in income. Tax exposure: approximately $50,000–$75,000 at the founder's marginal rate.
>
> **Scenario B — 2/3 inclusion rate (if the 2024 budget proposal was enacted — proposed effective June 25, 2024, for individual gains exceeding $250,000 annually):**
> A $300,000 capital gain × 2/3 = $200,000 included in income. Tax exposure: approximately $66,000–$100,000 at the founder's marginal rate.
>
> **In either scenario, the consequence is a material tax bill on a gain that does not correspond to any cash the founder has actually received.** The Section 85 election is required under both scenarios. The applicable rate affects only the magnitude of the exposure avoided — which under Scenario B is larger, making the election even more important, not less.
>
> The 2024 budget proposal was introduced in Bill C-69 and was in progress when Parliament was prorogued in January 2025. The accountant must confirm the currently applicable inclusion rate before the T2057 is filed.

Section 85(1) of the Income Tax Act provides a mechanism to transfer eligible property to a taxable Canadian corporation on a tax-deferred basis by making a joint election to transfer at a specified elected amount, which can be as low as the property's adjusted cost base (or $1 if the ACB is nil), deferring the capital gain until the founder disposes of the shares received.

## 3. Fair Market Value Determination

The elected amount must be within the range between the property's ACB (lower bound) and its FMV (upper bound). Setting the elected amount below ACB creates a deemed loss. Setting it above FMV creates a deemed benefit that may be taxable. For full tax deferral, the elected amount should equal the ACB.

| **Valuation Approach** | **Method** | **Pros** | **Cons** |
|-----------------------|-----------|---------|---------|
| Cost Approach | FMV = cost to recreate the IP from scratch at current market rates for developer time, research, and documentation | Simple; conservative; defensible if CRA challenges | Likely understates FMV; does not capture patent premium or market opportunity |
| Income Approach | FMV = NPV of projected future royalty streams discounted at a risk-adjusted rate | Most theoretically correct; captures market potential | Requires revenue projections that are inherently speculative at pre-revenue stage |
| Market Approach | FMV = comparable transaction multiples for fintech patent portfolios at pre-revenue stage | Market-grounded | Very limited comparable transaction data for this type of IP at this stage |
| Elected Amount Strategy | Elect at $1 to defer all gain; rely on cost-basis documentation to support low FMV | Maximum tax deferral; simplest approach | CRA may challenge if actual FMV is clearly much higher than $1; potential deemed benefit risk |

> **RECOMMENDATION FOR ACCOUNTANT:** The founder's preference is to elect at the lowest defensible amount to maximise tax deferral. Please advise whether a brief internal cost-basis documentation — logging the founder's time invested at a reasonable hourly rate — is sufficient protection against a CRA challenge, or whether a formal CBV valuation is required. A CBV valuation adds $5,000–$15,000 in cost that may be disproportionate at this stage.

## 4. Consideration Structure — Boot and Shares

In a Section 85 rollover, the corporation must issue at least one share as consideration. The consideration can also include boot — non-share consideration such as cash or a promissory note. The structure determines the tax consequences.

| **Consideration Component** | **Amount** | **Tax Treatment** | **Purpose** |
|----------------------------|-----------|------------------|-------------|
| **Common shares of Bridgepoint Intelligence Inc.** | FMV of property minus boot | ACB of shares equals elected amount. Capital gain deferred until shares are sold. | Core consideration — carries the deferred gain and provides equity ownership |
| Promissory Note (optional boot) | $nil — or an amount equal to ACB if ACB is greater than $nil | Boot received reduces the ACB of shares received. Boot up to the elected amount is tax-free; boot above elected amount triggers partial gain recognition. | Provides the founder with a creditor claim against the company equal to the ACB, allowing partial cash recovery without gain recognition |

If the founder's ACB in the transferred property is $nil, the recommended structure is: elected amount = $1; boot = $nil; consideration = common shares with nominal paid-up capital of $1. The founder receives shares with an ACB of $1 and the entire capital gain is deferred.

### 4.1 Paid-Up Capital Reduction

If the elected amount is less than the FMV of the property, the company must reduce the paid-up capital of the shares issued to equal the elected amount. Without this reduction, the difference between FMV and the elected amount could be deemed a dividend on distribution. The PUC reduction is automatic by operation of s. 85(2.1) when the T2057 election is filed.

## 5. T2057 Election Filing

The election is made jointly by the transferor and the transferee by filing Form T2057 — *Election on Disposition of Property by a Taxpayer to a Taxable Canadian Corporation* — with the Canada Revenue Agency.

| **T2057 Requirement** | **Detail** |
|----------------------|-----------|
| Who files | Both the founder (as transferor) and Bridgepoint Intelligence Inc. (as transferee) must sign. The company's first board resolution should authorise the CEO to sign the T2057 on behalf of the company. |
| Filing deadline | On or before the earlier of: (a) the filing due date for the founder's T1 return for the year of transfer (April 30, or June 15 if self-employed); and (b) the filing due date for the company's first T2 return (6 months after the company's first tax year end). |
| Late filing penalty | $100 per month or partial month late, to a maximum of $8,000 — and the CRA may deny the election entirely if filed more than 3 years late. **Do not file late.** |
| Property description | The T2057 requires a description of each property transferred. For IP, describe as: *"Intellectual property rights relating to the Liquidity Intelligence Platform, including provisional patent application rights, trade secrets, and know-how developed by the transferor prior to [DATE OF INCORPORATION]."* |
| Consideration description | List number and class of shares issued, stated capital, and amount of any boot. |
| Elected amount | Must be within range: ACB ≤ Elected Amount ≤ FMV. If ACB is nil, enter $1 as the elected amount — CRA will not accept $0. |

- **Board Resolution:** Pass a board resolution acknowledging the IP transfer, the Section 85 election, and the issuance of shares as consideration, to create a clean corporate record of the transaction.
